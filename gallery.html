🖼 Image Gallery

Physics demonstrations, portraits and classroom moments — click any image to enlarge

🧑‍🔬

Julius Sumner Miller at El Camino College, California, circa 1960s — a passionate physicist whose classroom demonstrations were legendary among students.

📺

Miller in the ABC television studio, Sydney, Australia — the set of "Why Is It So?" which aired for over two decades beginning in 1963.

🎓

Professor Miller receiving recognition from the Royal Institution. He was awarded the Michael Faraday Medal for science communication.

⚖

Miller demonstrating Newton's First Law (Inertia) — pulling a tablecloth from under dishes, illustrating that objects resist changes in their state of motion.

🌀

The gyroscope demonstration — a spinning wheel resists changes to its axis of rotation due to conservation of angular momentum.

🎳

Conservation of momentum illustrated with pendulum balls (Newton's Cradle) — energy and momentum transfer through a series of suspended steel spheres.

🔄

Miller spinning on a rotating stool while holding weights — extending arms slows rotation, pulling them in speeds it up (conservation of angular momentum).

🏋

The Atwood machine demonstration — two unequal masses hanging over a pulley reveal Newton's Second Law (F = ma) in a dramatic and measurable way.

🪣

Archimedes' principle in action — a submerged object displaces fluid equal to its volume. Miller would lower objects into water and measure the buoyant force directly.

🌬

Bernoulli's principle demonstration — blowing air between two hanging ping-pong balls causes them to attract rather than repel, due to reduced pressure in the airstream.

🧪

The collapsing can experiment — steam fills an aluminium can, which is then capped and cooled. Atmospheric pressure crushes the can inward as steam condenses.

💧

Surface tension demonstrated with a steel needle floating on water — the surface acts like an elastic skin strong enough to support the needle's weight.

🌊

Pascal's Law — pressure applied to a fluid in an enclosed container is transmitted equally in all directions. The hydraulic press is nature's own force multiplier.

🌈

White light dispersed through a prism into the visible spectrum — Newton's famous experiment showing that white light is composed of all colours of the rainbow.

🔭

Total internal reflection — light travelling through a curved glass rod demonstrates the principle behind optical fibres and periscopes.

🪞

Converging and diverging lenses — Miller illustrates how convex lenses focus light and concave lenses spread it, explaining the eye, telescope and camera.

⚡

The Van de Graaff generator in action — static electricity builds up to hundreds of thousands of volts, causing hair to stand on end and sparks to fly.

🧲

Electromagnetic induction — moving a magnet through a coil of wire generates an electric current. Miller demonstrates Faraday's breakthrough discovery with simple equipment.

🔋

Simple circuit demonstration — Miller builds a circuit with batteries, bulbs and switches, then traces the flow of current to illustrate Ohm's Law (V = IR).

🌡

Thermal expansion demonstration — a metal ball that barely fits through a ring at room temperature will not pass through when heated, showing solids expand with temperature.

❄

Liquid nitrogen experiments — a rubber ball submerged in liquid nitrogen (-196°C) becomes brittle and shatters, demonstrating the dramatic effects of extreme cold on material properties.

🔥

The fire syringe — compressing air rapidly in a cylinder heats the air enough to ignite a small piece of tinder inside, illustrating adiabatic compression.

〰

Standing waves on a string — the string vibrates in resonant modes, forming beautiful patterns of nodes and antinodes that reveal the harmonic nature of vibration.

🔔

Chladni figures — sand sprinkled on a vibrating metal plate forms intricate geometric patterns at the resonant frequencies, making sound waves visible.

🔊

The Doppler effect demonstrated with a rotating buzzer — the pitch rises as the buzzer approaches and falls as it recedes, exactly as predicted by wave theory.
